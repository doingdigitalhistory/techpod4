    • Exploring Twain’s Critique Through Four Key Works

    Each of these sources reveals a different facet of Twain’s attack on hypocrisy. From small-town life to global politics, Twain’s voice remains consistent: society often fails to live up to its own ideals.

    Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1884)

    Hypocrisy critiqued: Society claims to be “civilized” while upholding slavery. Huck’s moral struggle over helping Jim exposes the contradiction between social norms and true morality. Twain ridicules laws and religious teachings that justify injustice, showing how deeply hypocrisy is embedded in American culture.

    The Adventures of Tom Sawyer (1876)

    Hypocrisy critiqued: Adults enforce rules they themselves break, while children act more honestly. Twain critiques conformity and small-town norms, illustrating how “respectable” people often behave worse than mischievous children. This playful satire highlights the gap between appearance and reality in moral behavior.

    To the Person Sitting in the Darkness (1901)

    Hypocrisy critiqued: Twain attacks Western imperialism and missionary work, mocking the idea of “civilizing” foreign people while committing violence and theft. He exposes the moral contradictions of nations that preach virtue while exploiting others—a biting commentary on global politics.

    The United States of Lyncherdom (1901)

    Hypocrisy critiqued: Twain condemns a nation that claims freedom and justice while tolerating lynching and mob violence. This essay marks Twain’s shift from satire to blunt moral outrage, showing his growing frustration with systemic injustice.
