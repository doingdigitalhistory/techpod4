Introduction

Mark Twain, one of America’s most celebrated authors, was not only a humorist but also a sharp social critic. His works reveal a deep disdain for hypocrisy in society, politics, and morality. Through satire and biting commentary, Twain exposes contradictions between ideals and actions, challenging readers to question authority and cultural norms.

Our Argument

The primary sources of Mark Twain show his dislike for hypocrisy, which he demonstrates in his writings. In The Adventures of Tom Sawyer, adults preach virtue but reward misbehavior—a theme echoed in Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, where “civilized” society upholds slavery. Later essays like To the Person Sitting in the Darkness and The United States of Lyncherdom expand this critique to imperialism and mob violence, revealing Twain’s evolution from satire to moral outrage.

Why This Matters

Twain’s critique is timeless. Hypocrisy still shapes modern politics, social norms, and global relations. Understanding Twain’s perspective helps us reflect on our own society and the contradictions we tolerate today.
